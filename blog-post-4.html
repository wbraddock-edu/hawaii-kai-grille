Cooking with Taro: Hawaii's Sacred Root Vegetable

May 15, 2024 · Kai Grille Kitchen

In Hawaiian mythology, the taro plant — kalo — is the elder sibling of the Hawaiian people. The story goes that the sky father Wākea and his daughter Ho'ohōkūkalani had a stillborn child whose body was buried, and from that burial grew the first kalo plant. When Wākea and his earthly wife Papa had their next child, the first human being, he became the younger sibling of the taro plant. This mythology is not merely decorative — it reflects a profound relationship between the Hawaiian people and this plant that has sustained them for over a thousand years.

Taro's Nutritional Power

Kalo is not just culturally significant — it's extraordinarily nutritious. The corm (the underground stem we commonly call the root) is rich in complex carbohydrates, dietary fiber, potassium, vitamin E, vitamin B6, and manganese. It has a lower glycemic index than most staple starches, making it a more stable energy source than white rice or potatoes. The leaves are also edible and packed with vitamins A and C.

Taro must be cooked before eating — the raw corm contains calcium oxalate crystals that cause irritation to the mouth and throat. But once cooked, these break down completely, and the taro becomes mild, slightly sweet, and deeply satisfying.

How Taro is Used in Traditional Hawaiian Cooking

The most iconic preparation is poi — taro corms steamed or baked, then pounded with water to a smooth, slightly fermented paste. Poi is central to traditional Hawaiian feasts (lū'au), often eaten alongside kalua pig and lomi salmon. Fresh poi is sweet and mildly earthy; aged one to three days, it develops a pleasant tanginess similar to yogurt.

Laulau is another beloved preparation: taro leaves wrapped around pork, fish, or chicken and steamed in ti leaves until tender. The taro leaves contribute a silky, slightly mineral flavor that's completely different from the corm.

Modern Taro Preparations

At Kai Grille, we've explored taro across multiple preparations. Our purple taro risotto uses a varietyl called Bun Long, which has vivid purple flesh that turns a beautiful lavender when cooked with dairy. The flavor is earthier and slightly nuttier than Yukon Gold potato, and the color creates a striking presentation.

We also use taro purée as a base for several of our fish dishes, where its neutral sweetness provides a counterpoint to acidic sauces and bright garnishes. Taro chips are a natural snack application — thinly sliced and fried, they're crispy and more flavorful than potato chips.

Growing Taro on Maui

Traditional wetland taro cultivation — lo'i kalo — requires channeled water flowing through flooded paddies, similar to rice cultivation. The Wailuku and Waihe'e areas of Maui have historically been among the most productive taro-growing regions in Hawaii. Some lo'i kalo have been restored in recent years as part of cultural revitalization efforts, and supporting farms that grow taro traditionally is one way visitors and residents alike can honor Hawaii's agricultural heritage.

When you eat taro at Kai Grille, you're participating in a food tradition that predates Western contact in Hawaii by a thousand years. That depth of history is something we never take for granted, and it's why kalo will always be on our menu in some form.
