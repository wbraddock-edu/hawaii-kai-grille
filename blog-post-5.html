5 Incredible Ono Fish Recipes from Our Chef

May 2, 2024 · Kai Grille Kitchen

Ono — the Hawaiian word for "delicious" — is perhaps the most appropriately named fish in the ocean. This sleek, powerful member of the mackerel family inhabits the warm waters around Hawaii, and its firm, white, mild flesh makes it one of the most versatile fish you can cook with. Chef Kaimana Akana has been working with ono since before he opened Kai Grille, and over the years he's developed a repertoire of preparations that showcase its full potential.

Recipe 1: Kiawe-Grilled Ono with Lilikoi Beurre Blanc

This is our signature preparation and the one guests ask about most. Kiawe is a Hawaiian hardwood related to mesquite, and its smoke imparts a distinctive, slightly sweet character to anything cooked over it. For the beurre blanc, combine reduced white wine, shallots, and fresh lilikoi (passion fruit) juice, then mount cold butter in piece by piece off the heat for a sauce that's simultaneously rich and bright.

The key to grilling ono perfectly is a very hot grill, minimal movement, and restraint. Season simply with Hawaiian sea salt and a little oil, place skin-side down on oiled grates, and cook for 4 minutes without touching it. Flip once and cook 3 more minutes. The center should still be slightly translucent — it'll continue to cook off the heat.

Recipe 2: Ono Tacos with Mango Jalapeño Slaw

For something more casual and weeknight-friendly, ono tacos are unbeatable. Rub cubed ono with a blend of cumin, smoked paprika, garlic powder, and lime zest, then sear in a very hot cast iron pan. Make your slaw with shredded cabbage, fresh mango, sliced jalapeño, lime juice, and a touch of coconut cream instead of mayo. Serve in warm corn tortillas with pickled red onion and cilantro.

Recipe 3: Ono Ceviche with Coconut Leche de Tigre

The acid in citrus juice "cooks" the ono through denaturation without heat, producing a texture that's firm yet tender. Combine fresh lime juice, coconut milk, ginger, garlic, and a serrano chile to make a coconut leche de tigre. Dice ono into 1/2-inch cubes, add diced cucumber, red onion, and fresh herbs, and let it cure for 15-20 minutes in the tiger's milk. Serve immediately in a chilled bowl.

Recipe 4: Pan-Seared Ono with Brown Butter and Capers

This classic French technique translates beautifully to Hawaiian fish. Dust ono fillets in a light coating of flour seasoned with sea salt. Sear in clarified butter until golden on both sides. Remove the fish to rest and add fresh whole butter to the same pan. Swirl until it turns nutty and amber, then add capers, lemon juice, and fresh parsley. The sauce is ready in seconds — spoon it over the ono and serve immediately.

Recipe 5: Ono Crudo with Ponzu and Shiso

Raw preparations really allow ono's natural sweetness to shine. Slice sashimi-quality ono very thin across the grain and lay on a chilled plate. Make a quick ponzu by combining yuzu juice (or a mix of lemon and lime), soy sauce, mirin, and rice vinegar. Garnish with thinly sliced shiso, microgreens, toasted sesame seeds, and a few drops of good sesame oil. Serve immediately and eat within minutes of plating.

These five recipes represent the range of what ono can do — from rustic to refined, raw to roasted. The common thread is freshness and restraint: ono is delicious enough that it doesn't need to be buried under heavy flavors. Let the fish speak, and it will tell you something about the beautiful waters it came from.
