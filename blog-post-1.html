The Ultimate Guide to Hawaiian Fish Varieties

June 15, 2024 · Kai Grille Kitchen

If you've ever stood at a fish counter in Maui and felt overwhelmed by names like ono, opakapaka, and shutome, you're not alone. Hawaii's waters are home to some of the world's most extraordinary fish, and learning to navigate them is both a culinary and cultural education.

Ono (Wahoo)

The name says it all — ono means "delicious" in Hawaiian. This torpedo-shaped fish is a close relative of the king mackerel and is prized for its mild, sweet flavor and firm white flesh. Ono takes beautifully to high-heat preparations like grilling and searing, which is why it stars so prominently at Kai Grille. Its low fat content means it can dry out quickly, so we always cook it to just past translucent in the center.

Ono is a migratory fish found in tropical and subtropical waters worldwide, but Hawaii's version — pulled from clean Pacific waters — is considered especially pure-tasting. Local fishermen target ono using trolling lines, and the season peaks in summer, which is why you'll find it on our menu most frequently from May through August.

Ahi (Yellowfin Tuna)

Perhaps the most famous Hawaiian fish worldwide, ahi (yellowfin tuna) is the foundation of Hawaii's poke culture. At Kai Grille, we source our ahi from sustainable longline fisheries that operate under strict catch limits to protect the Pacific yellowfin population.

Ahi is extraordinary both raw — as in our poke trio — and seared rare over an extremely hot grill. The deep red flesh turns a beautiful pale pink when cooked, but the best preparations keep the interior ruby-red. Look for ahi with a bright, translucent appearance and a fresh, ocean smell when shopping at a market.

Mahi Mahi (Dorado/Dolphinfish)

Despite the "dolphin" nickname, mahi mahi are not dolphins — they're vibrantly colored fish known for their acrobatic leaps when caught. Mahi mahi has a mild, slightly sweet flavor with firm, pinkish-white flesh that lends itself to almost any cooking method. It's one of Hawaii's most sustainable catch options due to its rapid growth rate and shorter lifespan.

On Maui, mahi mahi is available year-round, though it's most abundant in spring. We love it pan-seared with a coconut lemongrass broth, which complements its delicate flavor without overwhelming it.

Opakapaka (Pink Snapper)

Considered the finest table fish in Hawaii, opakapaka is a deep-water pink snapper with lean, moist, delicately flavored flesh. It's more expensive than ono or mahi mahi, and for good reason — it lives at depths of 100–500 feet and is caught in relatively small quantities. When we put opakapaka on the menu as a special, it sells out quickly.

Opakapaka is excellent steamed, baked, or very gently sautéed. Its subtle sweetness should never be buried under heavy sauces — we prefer a simple preparation with fresh ginger, green onion, and a light shoyu dressing.

Shutome (Broadbill Swordfish)

Hawaii's swordfish is among the best in the world. The deep, cold waters around the Hawaiian Islands produce shutome with a clean, meaty flavor and dense texture that holds up beautifully to grilling. Hawaiian swordfish is typically caught by shallow-set longline, a method that dramatically reduces sea turtle bycatch — an important consideration for sustainable dining.

At Kai Grille, when shutome comes in as our daily catch, we prefer it simply grilled over kiawe wood and served with a bold citrus-butter sauce that matches the fish's intensity without masking its character.

How to Buy Fresh Hawaiian Fish

When buying fish at Maui's markets or fish counters, look for clear, bright eyes (if whole), firm flesh that springs back when pressed, and a fresh, clean ocean smell — never "fishy." The best fish markets in Wailuku will tell you the name of the boat that caught your fish and when it was brought in. This transparency is a hallmark of Hawaii's exceptional fishing community, and it's something we honor every day at Kai Grille.
