Farm-to-Table Dining on Maui: A Growing Movement

June 8, 2024 · Kai Grille Kitchen

Something remarkable is happening in the fields of Upcountry Maui. At elevations between 2,000 and 4,000 feet on the slopes of Haleakalā, a constellation of small farms is producing ingredients that are transforming the way we eat on this island — and changing the conversations happening in restaurant kitchens like ours.

The Upcountry Maui Difference

Upcountry Maui occupies a climatic sweet spot that's unique in Hawaii. The cooler temperatures, rich volcanic soil, and reliable rainfall create conditions where farmers can grow an extraordinary range of crops: crisp salad greens, sweet Kula strawberries, herbs, root vegetables, and even stone fruits that would struggle in lower elevations. The Kula Agricultural Park, a cooperative of small-scale farmers, has been central to building this agricultural identity.

For Kai Grille, this means a Tuesday morning run to the Maui Swap Meet or direct farm pickup that shapes the week's menu. When farmer Rodrigo Blanco brings in an exceptional flat of Persian cucumbers or an unusual variety of taro, that discovery becomes the inspiration for a dish — not the other way around.

The Farmers Behind Our Menu

Our relationship with Surfing Goat Dairy in Kula gave us access to handcrafted goat cheeses that appear as accents in our salads and on our cheese boards. Alex and Thomas Kafsack have been farming this land for decades, and their commitment to the goats' quality of life — pasture-raised, free-range — produces milk of exceptional richness and complexity.

For our herbs and specialty greens, we work with Kumu Farms, which pioneered organic farming on Maui and supplies some of the island's best restaurants. Their edible flowers and microgreens give our plates visual beauty that reflects the life force of ingredients grown with care.

Our purple taro comes from a multi-generational Hawaiian family in the Wailuku wetlands who have been cultivating kalo for over a century. Working with them isn't just a sourcing decision — it's a relationship that honors Hawaii's most sacred agricultural tradition.

Why It Matters

Before the farm-to-table movement took hold on Maui, most restaurants — even good ones — relied heavily on mainland and overseas suppliers for their produce and proteins. This wasn't just an environmental issue; it was a flavor issue. An asparagus flown from California to Maui loses a significant portion of its sugars and peak flavor within 24 hours of harvest.

When we shifted 80% of our produce to local sources, the difference was immediate and profound. Guests would ask: "What is this and why does it taste so different?" The answer was always the same: it was picked two days ago, not two weeks ago.

The Economic Case for Eating Local

Beyond the obvious environmental and flavor benefits, supporting Maui's farm economy keeps money circulating within our community. Studies show that dollars spent at locally owned farms and restaurants recirculate in the local economy at a significantly higher rate than money spent at national chains or on imported goods.

At Kai Grille, we pay fair prices to our farm partners — sometimes above market rate — because we believe the quality they produce, and the livelihoods they represent, are worth it. It's a philosophy of mutual investment that we're proud to embody.
